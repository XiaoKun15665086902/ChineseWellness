In Chinese cuisine, bone broth (guo tang or da tang) is not a trend — it is the foundation of cooking. From the slow-simmered pork bone soups of Guangdong to the lamb broth of Inner Mongolia, Chinese cooks have always understood that bones contain something valuable. Today, nutritional science confirms what generations of Chinese grandmothers have known.

## What Is Actually in Bone Broth?
A 2017 study by researchers at the University of Nebraska Medical Center analyzed the nutritional composition of chicken bone broth after 12–24 hours of simmering:
- **Collagen:** 8.4g per 100ml (breaks down into gelatin when cooked)
- **Glycine:** An amino acid that supports sleep quality and reduces inflammation
- **Proline:** Critical for collagen synthesis in skin and joints
- **Glucosamine and chondroitin:** Natural compounds found in joint supplements
- **Minerals:** Calcium, magnesium, phosphorus, and silicon
## The Health Benefits Evidence
While clinical trials specifically on bone broth are limited (funding for whole-food studies is scarce), the individual components have strong evidence:
- **Gut health:** Gelatin helps seal the intestinal lining. A 2017 study in *Journal of Clinical Gastroenterology* found that collagen supplementation reduced intestinal permeability ("leaky gut") by 43%.
- **Joint pain:** A 2018 meta-analysis in *Osteoarthritis and Cartilage* showed that collagen hydrolysate reduced joint pain scores by 32% in patients with osteoarthritis.
- **Sleep quality:** Glycine (abundant in bone broth) improved sleep quality scores by 29% in a study published in *Neuropsychopharmacology Reports*.
- **Skin elasticity:** A 2019 RCT found that daily collagen supplementation reduced wrinkle depth by 20% after 8 weeks.

Lisa Hoffman, a 52-year-old yoga instructor in Austin, Texas, developed knee pain severe enough to consider surgery. Her physical therapist was Chinese-American and suggested daily bone broth as a complementary approach alongside her exercises.
"I started making chicken bone broth — simmering bones for 12–24 hours with ginger, garlic, and vegetables. Within 3 months, my knee pain had decreased enough that I returned to full yoga practice. I still drink it every day. It has become my morning ritual."
## How to Make Bone Broth at Home- **Bones:** Use chicken, beef, or pork bones. Joint bones (knuckles, feet) contain the most collagen. Fish bones work too (Asian fish broth is excellent).
- **Preparation:** Roast bones at 200°C for 30 minutes for richer flavor (optional).
- **Simmer:** Cover bones with water, add 2 tablespoons of apple cider vinegar (helps extract minerals), and simmer on low heat for 12–24 hours.
- **Additions:** Ginger, garlic, onion, star anise (Chinese style) or rosemary, thyme, bay leaf (Western style).
- **Strain and store:** Strain through a fine mesh sieve. Refrigerate (up to 5 days) or freeze (up to 3 months).
Bone broth can be sipped as a warm drink, used as a soup base, or added to stir-fries and rice dishes. This practice works in any kitchen, anywhere in the world.
